# Section 3 — Waves

## Problem 1: Wave Properties

###  Conceptual Introduction

Every wave obeys one fundamental relationship:

$$v = f\lambda$$

Where:
- $v$ = wave speed — **set by the medium**, not the source
- $f$ = frequency — **set by the source**, does not change between media
- $\lambda$ = wavelength — **adjusts** when the medium changes

This means when the same sound moves from air into water, its frequency
stays at 440 Hz (the source hasn't changed), but the wavelength grows
because the wave travels faster in water.

---

### 📐 Given Information

| Symbol | Meaning | Value |
|--------|---------|-------|
| $f$ | Frequency | $440\ \text{Hz}$ |
| $v_{air}$ | Speed of sound in air | $343\ \text{m/s}$ |
| $v_{water}$ | Speed of sound in water | $1482\ \text{m/s}$ |

---

### 🔺 Core Formula

Rearranging $v = f\lambda$ to solve for wavelength:

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f}$$

---

### 🧮 Part A — Wavelength in Air

Substitute the values directly:

$$\lambda_{air} = \frac{v_{air}}{f} = \frac{343\ \text{m/s}}{440\ \text{Hz}}$$

$$\boxed{\lambda_{air} \approx 0.780\ \text{m}}$$

---

### 🧮 Part B — Wavelength in Water

The frequency stays the same (440 Hz) — only the wave speed changes:

$$\lambda_{water} = \frac{v_{water}}{f} = \frac{1482\ \text{m/s}}{440\ \text{Hz}}$$

$$\boxed{\lambda_{water} \approx 3.368\ \text{m}}$$

---

###  Sanity Check

The ratio of wavelengths must equal the ratio of speeds (since $f$ is constant):

$$\frac{\lambda_{water}}{\lambda_{air}} = \frac{v_{water}}{v_{air}} = \frac{1482}{343} \approx 4.32$$

$$\frac{3.368}{0.780} \approx 4.32 \quad \checkmark$$

> Sound in water has a wavelength **4.3 times longer** than in air at the
> same frequency. This makes sense — water is far less compressible than
> air, so the wave propagates faster, and each full cycle covers more distance.

---

###  Common Mistakes

- ❌ **Changing the frequency when changing medium** — frequency is always
  fixed by the source. Only $\lambda$ and $v$ change at the boundary.
- ❌ **Using $\lambda = vt$** — this gives distance, not wavelength.
  The correct rearrangement is $\lambda = v/f$.

---

###  What If the Frequency Were Doubled?

If $f = 880\ \text{Hz}$ (one octave higher), both wavelengths halve:

$$\lambda_{air} = \frac{343}{880} \approx 0.390\ \text{m}$$

$$\lambda_{water} = \frac{1482}{880} \approx 1.684\ \text{m}$$

Higher pitch = shorter wavelength. This is why low-frequency (bass) sounds
diffract around obstacles more easily — their wavelengths are comparable
to the size of everyday objects.
